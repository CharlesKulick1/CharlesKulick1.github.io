FAQ


What is expected from a DRP mentee?
The central part of your role as a DRP mentee will be to do independet study each week (2-4 hours) and meet with your mentor on a weekly basis to discuss mathematics. Weekly meetings with your mentor will be at least an hour long. During these you can ask any  questions that might pop up about your reading! Additionally, mentees need to attend a meet-and-greet information session at the beginning of the program, and give a short presentation about their work at the end of Spring quarter.


What kind of projects does DRP offer?
Each DRP mentor has a list of possible reading projects that are suitable for undergraduate students (see below). Most will involve reading through a textbook on a math topic that you probably haven't seen in your classes but that should be accessible to you after working through some background material. In contrast with a Research Experience for Undergraduates, conducting research is not the main goal of the DRP, though some of the mentors may have research projects accesible to you.


I already meet on a weekly basis to talk about math with a mentor, can we join the DRP?
Yes! In the application form both you and your mentor can specify they would like to work with each other. Having your meetings being part of the Directed Reading Program will give both of you the additional benefit of being part a nation-wide stablished program. There will also be events in which you and your mentor can share your work and experiences with other DRP participants.


I am not a mathematics major, can I still apply?
Yes! Anyone with a major in a math-related field is welcome to apply.


Sounds great! Anything else I should know?
The DRP is designed to allow undergraduates to get a feeling for advanced mathematics. This could help you answer questions like "what is math grad school like?", "is math research right for me?", or simply "what is it like to read math on your own?". Having individual time with a mentor is a great opportunity to deepen your understanding and enjoyment of mathematics!
​
More questions? Contact the UCSB-DRP at: drp@math.ucsb.edu

Daniel Halmrast

Research Interests: My work is in string theory, specifically studying the mysterious objects known as "D-branes" that arise in the theory. Such objects are difficult to study using conventional means, and recently the tools of algebraic geometry and homological algebra/category theory have been used to understand them better. My goal is to understand the dynamics of these objects when the spacetime they live in has a "quaternionic structure", whatever that means.
​
Potential Projects: Here are some potential projects and references that may or may not overlap; this should give you a rough idea of the sorts of things we could study.

Projects:
* What is spin? We attempt to answer this purely in linear algebra terms using the notion of a "spin structure" on an inner product space.
* What would a magnetic monopole look like? Trying to write down what the wavefunction of a magnetic monopole would be leads naturally to the idea of "line bundles" and "characteristic classes", with some surprising results.
* What are moduli spaces? We examine various notions of "similarity" between objects, and attempt to make the "space of all such objects, up to similarity" using some category theory and topology (and perhaps algebraic geometry)
Books:
* Donaldson's "Riemann Surfaces"
* Hatcher's "Algebraic Topology"
* Mukai's "Introduction to Invariants and Moduli"
* Riehl's "Category Theory in Context"
​* Mumford and Suominen's "Introduction to the Theory of Moduli" (Alg. Geo. background required)

Prerequisites: Math 111 sequence strongly recommended

Potential Projects: Depending on background and interest, some possible texts/projects would be:

"The Knot Book" by Adams (prereq: Math 8) Our main focus in this project is to understand what are Reidemeister moves. We will then cover a few knot invariants and use these invariants build a list of different knots.

The next project I have in mind is on elliptic curves. We will cover the first couple of chapters of "Rational Points on Elliptic Curves" by Joseph H. Silverman. "Introduction to Metric and Topological Spaces" by Wilson A. Sutherland (prereq: Math 8 and introduction to analysis)

 Charles Kulick

Research Interests: AI Agent Foundations/Safety
​
Potential Projects: You've probably seen the recent rise of AI generative models, with systems like GPT-3 and DALL-E 2 making conversation at nearly a human level and generating art to rival the human best in creativity. This is awesome, and this is also pretty concerning - as further advancements are made, we need to have solid mathematical foundations in order to ensure no catastrophic failures occur. We'll study some ideas of how to formalize agentic action in order to study and create safe agents. This area of math is very under-developed, and there are so many interesting ideas awaiting exploration that are available for excited and motived people to make an unbelievably large impact. We'll also have networking opportunities, potential conference attendances, and endless topics for original research moving forward!
